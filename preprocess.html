\n \n Wave–particle duality\n \n is the concept in\n \n quantum mechanics\n \n that every\n \n particle\n \n or\n \n quantic\n \n entity may be partly described in terms not only of particles, but also of\n \n waves\n \n . It expresses the inability of the\n \n classical\n \n concepts "particle" or "wave" to fully describe the behavior of\n \n quantum-scale\n \n objects. As\n \n Albert Einstein\n \n wrote:\n \n \n [1]\n \n \n

\n
\n

\n It seems as though we must use sometimes the one theory and sometimes the other, while at times we may use either. We are faced with a new kind of difficulty. We have two contradictory pictures of reality; separately neither of them fully explains the phenomena of light, but together they do.\n

\n
\n

\n Through the work of\n \n Max Planck\n \n ,\n \n Albert Einstein\n \n ,\n \n Louis de Broglie\n \n ,\n \n Arthur Compton\n \n ,\n \n Niels Bohr\n \n and many others, current scientific theory holds that all particles also have a wave nature (and vice versa).\n \n \n [2]\n \n \n This phenomenon has been verified not only for elementary particles, but also for compound particles like atoms and even molecules. For\n \n macroscopic\n \n particles, because of their extremely short wavelengths, wave properties usually cannot be detected.\n \n \n [3]\n \n \n

\n

\n Although the use of the wave-particle duality has worked well in physics, the\n \n meaning\n \n or\n \n interpretation\n \n has not been satisfactorily resolved; see\n \n Interpretations of quantum mechanics\n \n .\n

\n

\n Bohr regarded the "duality\n \n paradox\n \n " as a fundamental or metaphysical fact of nature. A given kind of quantum object will exhibit sometimes wave, sometimes particle, character, in respectively different physical settings. He saw such duality as one aspect of the concept of\n \n complementarity\n \n .\n \n \n [4]\n \n \n Bohr regarded renunciation of the cause-effect relation, or complementarity, of the space-time picture, as essential to the quantum mechanical account.\n \n \n [5]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Werner Heisenberg\n \n considered the question further. He saw the duality as present for all quantic entities, but not quite in the usual quantum mechanical account considered by Bohr. He saw it in what is called\n \n second quantization\n \n , which generates an entirely new concept of fields which exist in ordinary space-time, causality still being visualizable. Classical field values (e.g. the electric and magnetic field strengths of\n \n Maxwell\n \n ) are replaced by an entirely new kind of field value, as considered in\n \n quantum field theory\n \n . Turning the reasoning around, ordinary quantum mechanics can be deduced as a specialized consequence of quantum field theory.\n \n \n [6]\n \n \n \n \n [7]\n \n \n

\n \n Brief history of wave and particle viewpoints\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Democritus\n \n —the original\n \n \n atomist\n \n \n —argued that all things in the universe, including light, are composed of indivisible sub-components (light being some form of solar atom).\n \n \n [8]\n \n \n At the beginning of the 11th Century, the Arabic scientist\n \n Alhazen\n \n wrote the first comprehensive\n \n treatise on optics\n \n ; describing refraction, reflection, and the operation of a pinhole lens via rays of light traveling from the point of emission to the eye. He asserted that these rays were composed of particles of light. In 1630,\n \n René Descartes\n \n popularized and accredited the opposing wave description in his\n \n treatise on light\n \n , showing that the behavior of light could be re-created by modeling wave-like disturbances in a universal medium ("plenum"). Beginning in 1670 and progressing over three decades,\n \n Isaac Newton\n \n developed and championed his\n \n corpuscular hypothesis\n \n , arguing that the perfectly straight lines of\n \n reflection\n \n demonstrated light\'s particle nature; only particles could travel in such straight lines. He explained\n \n refraction\n \n by positing that particles of light accelerated laterally upon entering a denser medium. Around the same time, Newton\'s contemporaries\n \n Robert Hooke\n \n and\n \n Christiaan Huygens\n \n —and later\n \n Augustin-Jean Fresnel\n \n —mathematically refined the wave viewpoint, showing that if light traveled at different speeds in different media (such as water and air),\n \n refraction\n \n could be easily explained as the medium-dependent propagation of light waves. The resulting\n \n Huygens–Fresnel principle\n \n was extremely successful at reproducing light\'s behavior and was subsequently supported by\n \n Thomas Young\n \n \'s 1801 discovery of\n \n double-slit\n \n interference.\n \n \n [9]\n \n \n \n \n [10]\n \n \n The wave view did not immediately displace the ray and particle view, but began to dominate scientific thinking about light in the mid 19th century, since it could explain polarization phenomena that the alternatives could not.\n \n \n [11]\n \n \n

\n
\n
\n \n \n

\n

\n \n James Clerk Maxwell\n \n discovered that he could apply\n \n his equations for electromagnetism\n \n , which had been previously discovered, along with a slight modification to describe self-propagating waves of oscillating electric and magnetic fields. It quickly became apparent that visible light, ultraviolet light, and infrared light (phenomena thought previously to be unrelated) were all electromagnetic waves of differing frequency. The wave theory had prevailed—or at least it seemed to.\n

\n

\n While the 19th century had seen the success of the wave theory at describing light, it had also witnessed the rise of the\n \n atomic theory\n \n at describing matter.\n \n Antoine Lavoisier\n \n deduced the law of\n \n conservation of mass\n \n and categorized many new chemical elements and compounds; and\n \n Joseph Louis Proust\n \n advanced chemistry towards the atom by showing that elements combined in\n \n definite proportions\n \n . This led\n \n John Dalton\n \n to propose that elements were invisible sub components;\n \n Amedeo Avogadro\n \n discovered diatomic gases and completed the basic atomic theory, allowing the correct molecular formulae of most known compounds—as well as the correct weights of atoms—to be deduced and categorized in a consistent manner.\n \n Dimitri Mendeleev\n \n saw an order in\n \n recurring chemical properties\n \n , and created a\n \n table\n \n presenting the elements in unprecedented order and symmetry.\n

\n \n Particles of electricity\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n

\n At the close of the 19th century, the reductionism of atomic theory began to advance into the atom itself; determining, through physics, the nature of the atom and the operation of chemical reactions. Electricity, first thought to be a fluid, was now understood to consist of particles called\n \n electrons\n \n . This was first demonstrated by\n \n J. J. Thomson\n \n in 1897 when, using a\n \n cathode ray tube\n \n , he found that an electrical charge would travel across a vacuum (which would possess infinite resistance in classical theory). Since the vacuum offered no medium for an electric fluid to travel, this discovery could only be explained via a particle carrying a negative charge and moving through the vacuum. This\n \n electron\n \n flew in the face of classical electrodynamics, which had successfully treated electricity as a fluid for many years (leading to the invention of\n \n batteries\n \n ,\n \n electric motors\n \n ,\n \n dynamos\n \n , and\n \n arc lamps\n \n ). More importantly, the intimate relation between electric charge and electromagnetism had been well documented following the discoveries of\n \n Michael Faraday\n \n and\n \n James Clerk Maxwell\n \n . Since electromagnetism was\n \n known\n \n to be a wave generated by a changing electric or magnetic\n \n field\n \n (a continuous, wave-like entity itself) an atomic/particle description of electricity and charge was a\n \n non sequitur\n \n . Furthermore, classical electrodynamics was not the only classical theory rendered incomplete.\n

\n

\n \n Radiation quantization\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n
\n Main article:\n \n Planck\'s law\n \n
\n

\n In 1901,\n \n Max Planck\n \n published an analysis that succeeded in reproducing the observed\n \n spectrum\n \n of light emitted by a glowing object. To accomplish this, Planck had to make an ad hoc mathematical assumption of quantized energy of the oscillators (atoms of the\n \n black body\n \n ) that emit radiation. Einstein later proposed that electromagnetic radiation itself is quantized, not the energy of radiating atoms.\n

\n

\n \n Black-body radiation\n \n , the emission of electromagnetic energy due to an object\'s heat, could not be explained from classical arguments alone. The\n \n equipartition theorem\n \n of classical mechanics, the basis of all classical thermodynamic theories, stated that an object\'s energy is partitioned equally among the object\'s vibrational\n \n modes\n \n . But applying the same reasoning to the electromagnetic emission of such a thermal object was not so successful. That thermal objects emit light had been long known. Since light was known to be waves of electromagnetism, physicists hoped to describe this emission via classical laws. This became known as the\n \n black body\n \n problem. Since the equipartition theorem worked so well in describing the vibrational modes of the thermal object itself, it was natural to assume that it would perform equally well in describing the radiative emission of such objects. But a problem quickly arose: if each mode received an equal partition of energy, the short wavelength modes would consume all the energy. This became clear when plotting the\n \n Rayleigh–Jeans law\n \n which, while correctly predicting the intensity of long wavelength emissions, predicted infinite total energy as the intensity diverges to infinity for short wavelengths. This became known as the\n \n ultraviolet catastrophe\n \n .\n

\n

\n In 1900,\n \n Max Planck\n \n hypothesized that the frequency of light emitted by the black body depended on the frequency of the\n \n oscillator\n \n that emitted it, and the energy of these oscillators increased linearly with frequency (according to\n \n his constant\n \n \n h\n \n , where E = hν). This was not an unsound proposal considering that macroscopic oscillators operate similarly: when studying five\n \n simple harmonic oscillators\n \n of equal amplitude but different frequency, the oscillator with the highest frequency possesses the highest energy (though this relationship is not linear like Planck\'s). By demanding that high-frequency light must be emitted by an oscillator of equal frequency, and further requiring that this oscillator occupy higher energy than one of a lesser frequency, Planck avoided any catastrophe; giving an equal partition to high-frequency oscillators produced successively fewer oscillators and less emitted light. And as in the\n \n Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution\n \n , the low-frequency, low-energy oscillators were suppressed by the onslaught of thermal jiggling from higher energy oscillators, which necessarily increased their energy and frequency.\n

\n

\n The most revolutionary aspect of Planck\'s treatment of the black body is that it inherently relies on an integer number of oscillators in\n \n thermal equilibrium\n \n with the electromagnetic field. These oscillators\n \n give\n \n their entire energy to the electromagnetic field, creating a quantum of light, as often as they are\n \n excited\n \n by the electromagnetic field, absorbing a quantum of light and beginning to oscillate at the corresponding frequency. Planck had intentionally created an atomic theory of the black body, but had unintentionally generated an atomic theory of light, where the black body never generates quanta of light at a given frequency with an energy less than\n \n hν\n \n . However, once realizing that he had quantized the electromagnetic field, he denounced particles of light as a limitation of his approximation, not a property of reality.\n

\n \n Photoelectric effect illuminated\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n

\n While Planck had solved the ultraviolet catastrophe by using atoms and a quantized electromagnetic field, most contemporary physicists agreed that Planck\'s "light quanta" represented only flaws in his model. A more-complete derivation of black body radiation would yield a fully continuous and \'wave-like\' electromagnetic field with no quantization. However, in 1905\n \n Albert Einstein\n \n took Planck\'s black body model to produce his solution to another outstanding problem of the day: the\n \n photoelectric effect\n \n , wherein electrons are emitted from atoms when they absorb energy from light. Since their existence was theorized eight years previously, phenomenon had been studied with the electron model in mind in physics laboratories worldwide.\n

\n

\n In 1902\n \n Philipp Lenard\n \n discovered that the energy of these ejected electrons did\n \n not\n \n depend on the intensity of the incoming light, but instead on its\n \n frequency\n \n . So if one shines a little low-frequency light upon a metal, a few low energy electrons are ejected. If one now shines a very intense beam of low-frequency light upon the same metal, a whole slew of electrons are ejected; however they possess the same low energy, there are merely\n \n more of them\n \n . The more light there is, the more electrons are ejected. Whereas in order to get high energy electrons, one must illuminate the metal with high-frequency light. Like blackbody radiation, this was at odds with a theory invoking continuous transfer of energy between radiation and matter. However, it can still be explained using a fully classical description of light, as long as matter is quantum mechanical in nature.\n \n \n [12]\n \n \n

\n

\n If one used Planck\'s energy quanta, and demanded that electromagnetic radiation at a given frequency could only transfer energy to matter in integer multiples of an energy quantum\n \n hν\n \n , then the photoelectric effect could be explained very simply. Low-frequency light only ejects low-energy electrons because each electron is excited by the absorption of a single photon. Increasing the intensity of the low-frequency light (increasing the number of photons) only increases the number of excited electrons, not their energy, because the energy of each photon remains low. Only by increasing the frequency of the light, and thus increasing the energy of the photons, can one eject electrons with higher energy. Thus, using Planck\'s constant\n \n h\n \n to determine the energy of the photons based upon their frequency, the energy of ejected electrons should also increase linearly with frequency; the gradient of the line being Planck\'s constant. These results were not confirmed until 1915, when\n \n Robert Andrews Millikan\n \n , who had previously determined the charge of the electron, produced experimental results in perfect accord with Einstein\'s predictions. While the energy of ejected electrons reflected Planck\'s constant, the existence of photons was not explicitly proven until the discovery of the\n \n photon antibunching\n \n effect, of which a modern experiment can be performed in undergraduate-level labs.\n \n \n [13]\n \n \n This phenomenon could only be explained via photons, and not through any semi-classical theory (which could alternatively explain the photoelectric effect). When Einstein received his\n \n Nobel Prize\n \n in 1921, it was not for his more difficult and mathematically laborious\n \n special\n \n and\n \n general relativity\n \n , but for the simple, yet totally revolutionary, suggestion of quantized light. Einstein\'s "light quanta" would not be called\n \n photons\n \n until 1925, but even in 1905 they represented the quintessential example of wave-particle duality. Electromagnetic radiation propagates following linear wave equations, but can only be emitted or absorbed as discrete elements, thus acting as a wave and a particle simultaneously.\n

\n \n Treatment in modern quantum mechanics\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n

\n Wave–particle duality is deeply embedded into the foundations of\n \n quantum mechanics\n \n . In the\n \n formalism\n \n of the theory, all the information about a particle is encoded in its\n \n \n wave function\n \n \n , a complex-valued function roughly analogous to the amplitude of a wave at each point in space. This function evolves according to a\n \n differential equation\n \n (generically called the\n \n Schrödinger equation\n \n ). For particles with mass this equation has solutions that follow the form of the wave equation. Propagation of such waves leads to wave-like phenomena such as interference and diffraction. Particles without mass, like photons, have no solutions of the Schrödinger equation so have another wave.\n

\n

\n The particle-like behavior is most evident due to phenomena associated with\n \n measurement in quantum mechanics\n \n . Upon measuring the location of the particle, the particle will be forced into a more localized state as given by the uncertainty principle. When viewed through this formalism, the measurement of the wave function will randomly "\n \n collapse\n \n ", or rather "\n \n decohere\n \n ", to a sharply peaked function at some location. For particles with mass the likelihood of detecting the particle at any particular location is equal to the squared amplitude of the wave function there. The measurement will return a well-defined position, (subject to\n \n uncertainty\n \n ), a property traditionally associated with particles. It is important to note that a measurement is only a particular type of interaction where some data is recorded and the measured quantity is forced into a particular\n \n eigenstate\n \n . The act of measurement is therefore not fundamentally different from any other interaction.\n

\n

\n Following the development of\n \n quantum field theory\n \n the ambiguity disappeared. The field permits solutions that follow the wave equation, which are referred to as the wave functions. The term particle is used to label the irreducible representations of the\n \n Lorentz group\n \n that are permitted by the field. An interaction as in a\n \n Feynman diagram\n \n is accepted as a calculationally convenient approximation where the outgoing legs are known to be simplifications of the propagation and the internal lines are for some order in an expansion of the field interaction. Since the field is non-local and quantized, the phenomena which previously were thought of as paradoxes are explained. Within the limits of the wave-particle duality the quantum field theory gives the same results.\n

\n \n Albert Einstein\n \n , who, in his search for a\n \n Unified Field Theory\n \n , did not accept wave-particle duality, wrote:\n \n \n [46]\n \n \n

\n
\n

\n This double nature of radiation (and of material corpuscles) ... has been interpreted by quantum-mechanics in an ingenious and amazingly successful fashion. This interpretation ... appears to me as only a temporary way out...\n

\n
\n

\n The\n \n many-worlds interpretation\n \n (MWI) is sometimes presented as a waves-only theory, including by its originator,\n \n Hugh Everett\n \n who referred to MWI as "the wave interpretation".\n \n \n [47]\n \n \n

\n

\n The\n \n \n three wave hypothesis\n \n \n of R. Horodecki relates the particle to wave.\n \n \n [48]\n \n \n \n \n [49]\n \n \n The hypothesis implies that a massive particle is an intrinsically spatially, as well as temporally extended, wave phenomenon by a nonlinear law.\n

\n

\n \n Particle-only view\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n

\n Still in the days of the\n \n old quantum theory\n \n , a pre-quantum-mechanical version of wave–particle duality was pioneered by\n \n William Duane\n \n ,\n \n \n [50]\n \n \n and developed by others including\n \n Alfred Landé\n \n .\n \n \n [51]\n \n \n Duane explained diffraction of\n \n x-rays\n \n by a crystal in terms solely of their particle aspect. The deflection of the trajectory of each diffracted photon was explained as due to\n \n quantized momentum transfer\n \n from the spatially regular structure of the diffracting crystal.\n \n \n [52]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Neither-wave-nor-particle view\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n

\n It has been argued that there are never exact particles or waves, but only some compromise or intermediate between them. For this reason, in 1928\n \n Arthur Eddington\n \n \n \n [53]\n \n \n coined the name "\n \n wavicle\n \n " to describe the objects although it is not regularly used today. One consideration is that zero-dimensional\n \n mathematical points\n \n cannot be observed. Another is that the formal representation of such points, the\n \n Dirac delta function\n \n is unphysical, because it cannot be\n \n normalized\n \n . Parallel arguments apply to pure wave states.\n \n Roger Penrose\n \n states:\n \n \n [54]\n \n \n

\n
\n

\n "Such \'position states\' are idealized wavefunctions in the opposite sense from the momentum states. Whereas the momentum states are infinitely spread out, the position states are infinitely concentrated. Neither is normalizable [...]."\n

\n
\n

\n \n Relational approach to wave–particle duality\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Relational quantum mechanics\n \n has been developed as a point of view that regards the event of particle detection as having established a relationship between the quantized field and the detector. The inherent ambiguity associated with applying Heisenberg’s uncertainty principle is consequently avoided; hence there is no wave-particle duality.\n \n \n [55]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Applications\n \n \n \n [\n \n \n edit\n \n \n ]\n \n \n

\n

\n Although it is difficult to draw a line separating wave–particle duality from the rest of quantum mechanics, it is nevertheless possible to list some applications of this basic idea.\n

\n
    \n
  • \n Wave–particle duality is exploited in\n \n electron microscopy\n \n , where the small wavelengths associated with the electron can be used to view objects much smaller than what is visible using visible light.\n
    • \n
  • \n Similarly,\n \n neutron diffraction\n \n uses neutrons with a wavelength of about 0.1\n \n nm\n \n , the typical spacing of atoms in a solid, to determine the structure of solids.\n
    • \n
  • \n Photos are now able to show this dual nature, which may lead to new ways of examining and recording this behaviour.\n \n \n [56]\n \n \n
    • \n
